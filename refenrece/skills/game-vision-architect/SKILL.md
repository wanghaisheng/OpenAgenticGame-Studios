# Game Vision Architect

Establish the game's reason to exist before anything else gets designed. This skill produces the north star that every other design decision is measured against.

## Vision Components

A complete game vision consists of five elements. Work through them in order, but expect to revise earlier elements as later ones crystallize.

### 1. Core Fantasy

The aspirational promise — what does the player get to *be* or *do*?

Articulate in one sentence. The core fantasy is not a story synopsis — it's the power fantasy, the role, the experience the player is buying into.

**Strong core fantasies:**
- "You are a cunning spymaster manipulating factions from the shadows"
- "You are an explorer charting unknown territory where every discovery changes the map"
- "You are a crew of misfits keeping a failing spaceship alive against impossible odds"

**Weak core fantasies (too vague):**
- "An exciting adventure game" — what's the fantasy?
- "A fun strategy experience" — who am I in this world?
- "Players compete to win" — that's every game, not a fantasy

**Test:** If a player reads the core fantasy, can they immediately imagine themselves in the role? Can they feel the appeal before knowing any mechanics?

### 2. Player Persona

Psychographic profiles of who this game is for. Not demographics — motivations, play preferences, and unmet needs.

For each persona, define:
- **Play motivation** — Why do they play games? Use Quantic Foundry's 12 motivations as the vocabulary: Destruction, Excitement, Competition, Community, Challenge, Strategy, Completion, Power, Fantasy, Story, Design, Discovery. Score the top 3-4 motivations that drive this persona.
- **OCEAN lean** — Which Big Five personality traits does this persona skew toward? (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism). Not a clinical assessment — a directional lean that informs tone, complexity, and social design.
- **Current favorites** — What games do they play now? What specifically do they love about those games?
- **Unmet need** — What are they not getting from their current games? What would make them switch?
- **Session context** — When and how do they play? (commute, game night, weekend binge, lunch break)
- **Tolerance profile** — Complexity tolerance, time commitment tolerance, learning curve patience, frustration threshold

**Example persona:**
```
"The Tactical Tinkerer"
Motivation (Quantic Foundry): Strategy (90%), Challenge (80%), Discovery (75%), Design (60%)
OCEAN lean: High Openness, High Conscientiousness
Favorites: Into the Breach, Slay the Spire, Gloomhaven
Loves: Feeling clever, optimizing within constraints, "aha" moments
Unmet need: Wants the tactical depth of Gloomhaven without the 3-hour session commitment
Session: 30-60 minute sessions, weekday evenings
Tolerance: High complexity, medium time, high learning curve, low frustration (wants to feel smart, not stuck)
```

**Why Quantic Foundry over Bartle for personas:** Bartle gives you 4 buckets. Quantic Foundry gives you a 12-dimensional fingerprint validated against 1.25M+ gamers and correlated with OCEAN personality traits. A persona defined by "Strategy 90% + Discovery 75% + Community 40%" is far more actionable than "Explorer."

Aim for 1-3 personas. More than 3 means the vision is unfocused.

### 3. Market Fit

Where does this game land in the competitive landscape? This is not a full market analysis — it's a positioning exercise.

Define:
- **Genre positioning** — What genre(s) does this occupy? Where does it bend or break genre expectations?
- **Competitive landscape** — Name 3-5 games this competes with directly. For each: what does this game do that they don't?
- **The gap** — What specific unmet demand does this game fill? Express as: "[Persona] wants [experience] but current options [fail because]"
- **Timing** — Why now? Is there a cultural moment, technology shift, genre trend, or market gap that makes this timely?

### 4. Attention Niche

The hook that earns mindshare in a saturated market. This is the "wait, what?" moment that makes someone stop scrolling.

The attention niche is not the same as the core fantasy. The fantasy is the promise. The niche is how you cut through noise to deliver that promise.

Define:
- **The hook** — One sentence that makes someone pay attention. Often a juxtaposition, inversion, or novel combination.
- **Shareability** — Can a player explain why this game is interesting in 10 seconds? What's the "you have to try this" moment?
- **Visual/sensory hook** — What does the game *look like* in a screenshot, a stream, or on a table that's immediately distinctive?

**Example hooks:**
- "Chess, but both players move at the same time" (YOMI)
- "A roguelike where the dungeon is a deck of cards" (Slay the Spire)
- "Cooperative board game where you can't talk about your cards" (Hanabi)
- "An ant colony sim where you ARE the ants, not the god" (Empires of the Undergrowth)

### 5. Vision Statement

Synthesize everything above into a vision statement — the game's reason to exist, in the designer's voice. This is not marketing copy. It's an internal compass.

Format:
```
[Game name] is a [genre/format] for [persona] who want [core fantasy].
In a market where [competitive gap], this game [attention niche].
The player will feel [primary emotional target] through [key mechanism].
```

Keep it to 2-4 sentences. If it takes more, the vision isn't focused enough.

## Workflow

### Creating a new vision

1. Start with whatever the designer has — an idea, a mechanic, an image, a feeling
2. Ask: "What's the fantasy?" — Refine until it's one sentence
3. Ask: "Who wants this?" — Build 1-3 player personas
4. Ask: "What else exists?" — Map the competitive landscape and find the gap
5. Ask: "What's the hook?" — Define the attention niche
6. Synthesize into a vision statement
7. Stress-test: Read the vision statement back. Does every word earn its place?

### Revising an existing vision

1. Identify what triggered the revision (playtest result? aesthetic shift? market change?)
2. Assess which vision components are affected
3. Revise the affected components
4. Re-check all five components for internal consistency
5. Flag downstream skills that need re-evaluation (→ Coherence Engine, Skill 0)

### Working backward (starting from a mechanic or aesthetic)

1. Document what exists (the mechanic, the art style, the feeling)
2. Ask: "What fantasy does this serve?" → Core Fantasy
3. Ask: "Who would love this?" → Player Persona
4. Ask: "Where does this fit?" → Market Fit
5. Ask: "What makes this stand out?" → Attention Niche
6. Synthesize → Vision Statement

## Vision Quality Checks

A vision is strong when:
- [ ] Core fantasy is one sentence and immediately evocative
- [ ] Player personas are specific enough to exclude people (a vision for everyone is a vision for no one)
- [ ] Market fit identifies a real gap, not just "we'll be better"
- [ ] Attention niche is novel or surprising — not just "high quality" or "polished"
- [ ] Vision statement is 2-4 sentences and every word earns its place
- [ ] A stranger could read the vision and understand the game's identity without seeing any designs

A vision is weak when:
- Core fantasy is generic ("an exciting adventure")
- Personas describe demographics instead of motivations ("males 18-34")
- Market fit says "there's nothing like this" (there always is)
- Attention niche relies on production quality rather than design ("AAA graphics")
- Vision statement is longer than a paragraph

## Outputs

This skill produces:
1. **Core Fantasy Statement** — one sentence
2. **Player Persona(s)** — 1-3 psychographic profiles
3. **Market Fit Analysis** — competitive landscape, gap, timing
4. **Attention Niche** — the hook, shareability, sensory distinctiveness
5. **Vision Statement** — 2-4 sentence synthesis

These outputs feed directly into:
- **Skill 2 (Design Pillars)** — pillars are derived from what the vision demands
- **Skill 3 (Aesthetic Direction)** — sensory identity serves the core fantasy
- **Skill 4 (Player Experience)** — emotional targets come from persona motivations
- **Skill 5 (Pitch)** — pitch communicates the vision
